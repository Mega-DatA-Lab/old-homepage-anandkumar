Learning of Graphical Models

Overview: A graphical model, also known as a Markov random field is a succinct representation of a multivariate distribution, where the conditional independence relationships are represented as a graph. Mathematically, two nodes are non-neighbors in the dependency graph iff. their data are statistical independent, conditioned on the data at all the other nodes.

A major challenge is that the graph structure of the graphical model is typically unknown and has to be learnt from samples. The goal is to obtain consistent algorithms which output the correct structure as the number of samples goes to infinity. Typically, the data is high dimensional, meaning that the data dimensionality is comparable to the number of samples. Hence, it is desirable to have algorithms with low sample complexity. Computational complexity is another performance criterion. Learning a general graphical model is NP-hard and it is of interest to find classes of graphs where learning is tractable.

High Dimensional Structure Learning of Ising Models on Sparse Random Graphs
(Submitted to the Annals of Statistics.)

Algorithms for learning structure of Ising models on sparse random graphs are proposed in the regime of correlation decay. Lower bounds on sample complexity for consistent structure learning are established.

Learning Latent Tree Graphical Models (Submitted to Journal of Machine Learning Research, available on Arxiv, Sept. 2010.)

Learning latent tree models where some nodes are hidden is considered. Algorithms with guaranteed results are proposed.

Learning High-Dimensional Markov Forest Distributions: Analysis of Error Rates (Submitted to Journal of Machine Learning Research, available on Arxiv, 2010)

Analysis of algorithm to learn forest-structured discrete graphical models. The key is to design sample size based threshold to remove weak edges from the Chow-Liu tree.
Learning Gaussian Tree Models: Analysis of Error Exponents and Extremal Structures (IEEE Tran. on Signal Processing, 2010)
Extremal tree structures which are easiest and hardest to learn from samples are characterized.
A Large-Deviation Analysis for the Maximum Likelihood Learning of Tree Structures (submitted to IEEE Tran. on Information Theory )
Rate of exponential decay of error in learning the correct tree of a graphical model is analyzed.

Inference in Graphical Models

Overview: In this line of work, graphical models are employed to model relationships between data collected at different nodes in the networks such as the sensor networks. The end goal is to undertake distributed statiscal inference about the environment using the samples. In-network computation schemes which incorporate the graphical model information are considered and the scalability of these schemes as the size of the network grows is studied.

Detection Error Exponent for Spatially Dependent Samples in Random Networks (Proc. of IEEE ISIT, 2009)
Error exponent for hypothesis testing of Markov random field observations in randomly placed nodes
Prize-Collecting Data Fusion for Cost-Performance Tradeoff in Distributed Inference (Proc. of IEEE INFOCOM, 2009)
A novel formulation for sensor selection and in-network fusion for distributed inference known as the prize-collecting data fusion (PCDF) is proposed.

Cost-Performance Tradeoff in Multi-hop Aggregation for Statistical Inference (Proc. of IEEE International Symposium on Information Theory, 2008)
The problem of tradeoff between performance and energy consumption in a sensor network for a statistical inference application is considered.

Energy Scaling Laws for Distributed Inference in Random Networks (IEEE J. Selec. Area Comm., Sept. 2009)
Scaling laws are derived for average energy consumption for data aggregation for statistical inference.

Routing for Statistical Inference in Sensor Networks (Handbook on Array Processing and Sensor Networks, 2009)
Overview of our approach to conduct in-network processing of the likelihood function which is the minimal sufficient statistic and deliver it to the fusion center for inference.

Minimum Cost Data Aggregation with Localized Processing for Statistical Inference (Proc. of IEEE INFOCOM, 2008)
The problem of minimum cost in-network fusion of measurements, collected from distributed sensors via multihop routing is considered.

Optimal Node Density for Detection in Energy Constrained Random Networks (IEEE Trans. Signal Proc., 2008)
The problem of optimal node density maximizing the Neyman-Pearson detection error exponent subject to a constraint on average (per node) energy consumption is analyzed.

Detection of Gauss-Markov Random Fields with Nearest-Neighbor Dependency (IEEE Trans. Information Theory, vol. 56, no. 2, Feb. 2009)
The problem of hypothesis testing against independence for a Gauss-Markov random field (GMRF) is analyzed and a closed-form error exponent is derived.

Adaptive Learning and Distributed Access

Overview: The problem of adaptive learning of channel availability and medium access control among multiple secondary users in a cognitive radio network is considered. Due to lack of coordination and information exchange among the users and due to unknown channel availabilities, the cognitive users experience regret and efficient decentralized algorithms are designed with order-optimal sum regret. This involves multi-armed bandits with multiple users.

Distributed Learning and Allocation of Cognitive Users with Logarithmic Regret (Accepted to IEEE JSAC on Advances in Cognitive Radio Networking and Communications, 2009)
Learning and allocation of secondary users in a cognitive network is analyzed.
 
Opportunistic Spectrum Access with Multiple Users: Learning under Competition (Proc. of IEEE INFOCOM, 2010)
Distributed learning of unknown channel availability statistics and allocation of multiple secondary users in a cognitive network is analyzed. Schemes with logarithmic regret are proposed.

MAC Design for Inference

Overview: The design of scalable medium access control and channel computation algorithms for statistical inference applications is considered. Here, scalability is in terms of having low bandwidth requirements with network size. Traditionally, MAC schemes allocate transmission from different nodes to orthogonal channels (such as in time or frequency) to avoid interference. Instead, we propose a MAC scheme where nodes may interfere with one another, yet achieve good inference accuracy in the end. We allocate orthogonal channels to data levels: all nodes reaching the same local decision use the same orthogonal channel to transmit, if they decide to do so.

Distributed Estimation Via Random Access (Information Theory, IEEE Transactions on, 2008)
Type-based random access (TBRA) scheme for distributed estimation in wireless-sensor networks.
Type-Based Random Access for Distributed Detection over Multiaccess Fading Channels (IEEE Trans. Signal Proc., 2007)
Design of random-access schemes for distributed detection in wireless-sensor networks. We present an optimal spatio-temporal type-based random access (TBRA) scheme with an energy constraint, which maximizes the asymptotic performance. (2008 IEEE Signal Proc. Society Young Author Best Paper Award).
A Large Deviation Analysis of Detection over Multi-Access Channels with Random Number of Sensors (Proc. of ICASSP'06, 2006)
We characterize the performance in terms of error exponents assuming a random number of sensors transmitting their observations using Type Based Multiple Access, as the mean number of sensors goes to infinity. (Best Paper Award).

Network Monitoring Using Timestamps

Overview: I spent two summers at IBM Watson Research and worked on network monitoring problems relevant to managing financial cyber-infrastructure. Automated fine-grained monitoring solutions are needed to manage these large, complex and heterogeneous networks. We established a mathematical framework for probabilistic tracking of packets using only timestamps with no access to packet payload or packet identifier. We proposed scalable monitoring algorithms with decentralized implementation which are provably optimal for monitoring.

Tracking in a Spaghetti Bowl: Monitoring Transactions Using Footprints (Proc. ACM Intl. Conf. on Measurement & Modeling of Computer Systems (Sigmetrics), 2008)
Monitoring end-to-end service-level transactions in an enterprise environment in the absence of instrumentation support. This leads to maximum likelihood association between the footprints (log-records) and the transactions in the system.

Seeing Through Black Boxes : Tracking Transactions through Queues under Monitoring Resource Constraints (Submitted to Elsevier Performance Evaluation, 2010) Strategies to maximize monitoring accuracy using timestamps is analyzed.
